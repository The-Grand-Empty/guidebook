Almost every interstellar spaceship in The Grand Empty was built on a budget paid for through some form of securities fraud; in fact, you can often guess which sort, too, purely based on the ship's design. If you can understand _why_ that is, that will teach you almost everything you need to know about the Grand Empty.

# Locality
There is no faster-than-light in the Grand Empty --- not even FTL communication. Advanced engines may allow a vessel to accelerate to reletavistic speeds so that its crew spend only a month aboard to reach a star fifty light-years away -- but the information available to use to select it and decide what cargo to carry was already at best fifty years old, the planet will be another fifty years older still by the time they arrive. A _lot_ changes in a hundred years, and if --- _if_ --- they decide to return, the home they left will already be a hundred years in the past when they get back.

When a crew predicts poorly, ships can and do end up declaring bankruptcy. They have run out of supplies and with their misjudged arbitrage cannot fund the repairs the ship needs --- the only thing left to sell might be the ship itself, or perhaps just a few of its berths if some of the crew can be convinced to retire (euphemistically or not).

When a crew predicts well, though, it can be quite lucrative --- but why would the crew return home?

Nobody can project military might or fight a war across a hundred-year gap --- heck, you can't even _aim a missile_ across that kind of gap. Various warmongering regimes have occasionally tried, and not always to _no_ effect; but in general, exoplanets are simply too small, their stars too far away, and their orbits too unpredictable. Even a guided missile, at relativistic speeds, sees relativistic effects that cut into its maneuvering authority and the resolving power of its guidance sensors --- and anything slower than that can be cheaply and easily shot down if the recipient is even slightly paying attention.

It's not impossible for a crew to remain loyal, but it's not a given. The math of relativity ensures there can be no extra incentive for it, even for the captain, and there are always a million and one other stars that would be more profitable and less problematic to travel onward to, into the Grand Empty!

## Relativistic Interest Spread
For those in a position to benefit directly, i.e. crew, when arbitrage works out, their share of the spoils can be quite lucrative for a month's work. And even when it isn't, they're still somewhere new, seeing a bit of the universe they might never have gotten to.

But, even if the ship did return, it would never be possible to amortize the capital investment the ship represents to anyone _not_ aboard.

For the sake of simplicity we'll assume that a trade ship's home system does _not_ experience a major regime change during the ship's journey (though this is actually fairly unlikely, for reasons explored later). If so, though, it's reasonable to expect a steady baseline rate of economic growth during the ship's 100 year round trip -- say, extra-conservatively, that a planet-side investment made with that same capital yields 2% per year. Over the 100 year tour, that investment will gain +624% growth grow to 7.24% its original size.

In general I like to keep things slower-than-light because of the way that that gives different locations a sense of being well-and-truly _disconnected_ from most anywhere else.

With relativistic drive systems capable of making the travel times somewhat tolerable for those on ships, but making travel almost completely irrelevant to the lives of anyone planet-side.

I've got a whole socio-economic theory built up of how interstellar trade and travel would work in a strictly slower-than-light world (i.e. without even FTL comms)

Where, it's next to impossible for a planet-based organization to project any kind of power at all beyond its home system, and in fact it would never even be remotely profitable for such an organization to try to send trade ships to other stars. The only interstellar trade or travel happens at the behest of more-or-less independent ships that are soverign only to their own crews, doing their best to guess what sorts of arbitrage might exist for them between where they are now and some future planet, based on information about other planets that is, at its newest, decades old, and will be even more decades old by the time their ship actually gets there.

(Tho I'm happy to entertain other theories of interstellar commerce using suitable FTL tech to enable them, depending on what we want.)

But under my STL theory, there's really only a handful of ways to feasibly finance constructing and operating an interstellar-capable ship. The first is ticket pre-sales, to people who would want to be on said ship. Often including things like 'lottery class' tickets, government-funded universal lottery, or sometimes just outright scams -- though the last option doesn't really even work for all ship classes either.

Colony ships are one category -- meant either for green-field colonization on uninhabited worlds, or more often co-colonization of worlds believed to have only small colonies that haven't reached planet scale. Non-relativistic generational or cryptosleeper colony ships can't usually get away with not making good on at least _most_ of the tickets sold and loans taken to pay for it, either -- since enforcement efforts to stop that kind of departure _can_ usually catch up and make good on legal consequences.

The usual price-point for a given class of ticket is always more-or-less the entire net worth of the average individual meant to purchase it; and the ships are sometimes even financed directly, with the remaining planet-side creditors for the ship-building taking ownership of passengers' remaining planet-side estates when they board.

Colony efforts appeal far more to the disenfranchised than the uber-rich, and the baseline cost is usually too large for a ship project to even have a large enough market cap to succeed when only targeting the uber-rich. So to make financing work at a per-bunk price reachable for that target demographic requires a ship that's absolutely enormous, just to get the sorts of economies of scale that can make financing work.

(i.e. the real reason colony ships have millions of colonists isn't for genetic diversity, its about money; even if the former is still a neat side-benefit.)

Smaller relativistic ships are financed similarly to start, with a smaller number of much costlier tickets, but the lion's share of funding comes from massive financial fraud. At its least fraudulent, might be selling overhyped shares of any successful return cargo, to be traded indefinitely planet-side by people sure that the ship'll be back any day now, or at least sure that they can convince the next sucker of that in fifty years.
Trade-ship bonds are a lot more blatantly fraudulent, when you realize that the plausible-sounding 1% APY on those bonds would have to correspond to a relativistic ship-side rate of more like 10,000% APY for the ship to be able to pay them when it gets back hundreds or thousands of planet-side years later, after having been gone for only one year in its own reference frame.

Such ships are always behind schedule on construction, up until the moment the crew absconds with it and defaults on any remaining creditors.

At its most legit, a relativistic trade ship will have been prevented from leaving and forced to declare bankruptcy, and then the creditors will have sold it to the crew that ends up leaving with it as a way to try to recover as much of their investment as possible.

Few planetary civilizations ever end up completing construction on more than one or two -- making essentially no two ships alike.

The financing situation is also often why trade ships often have such motley crews with so many different species -- because, this is how a ship gets itself out of bankruptcy, when it arrives somewhere, doesn't have enough fuel or supplies to leave, and doesn't have any cargo valuable enough to sell to the locals to be able to buy it. Currency is totally non-portable, and about the only commodity that can even _almost_ be relied upon to hold value is highly-enriched nuclear material. Information trade -- pirating every single piece of media from the entire last planet's catalog and bringing it with you to sell to the next planet -- also helps a bit with stability, but when push comes to shove, a ship crew will draw lots for who's gonna be forced to retire to this planet, and sell the vacated bunk for the effective net worth of some new crewmate who wants to join.

Usually, the majority species on a given trade ship will be the species from whichever planet the ship was most recently forced to declare bankruptcy at anyway -- with just everyone retiring and selling off the ship to fund this, and then whatever local organization it ends up in the hands of, refurbishing it and selling it to a new crew (though often hiring on at least some of the old crew again for the sake of training them in how all its systems work).
